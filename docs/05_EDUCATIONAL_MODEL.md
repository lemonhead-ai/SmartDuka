# 05 · Educational Model

---

## Philosophy

Smart Duka is not a curriculum delivery tool. It is a **situated learning environment** — a place where skills are acquired through authentic practice in a recognisable context, not through explicit instruction followed by assessment.

The theoretical foundation draws from three established frameworks:

1. **Situated Learning Theory** (Lave & Wenger, 1991) — knowledge is most durably acquired when it is embedded in authentic activity within a community of practice. For East African children, the duka is that authentic activity.

2. **Zone of Proximal Development** (Vygotsky) — learning occurs most efficiently when the challenge is just beyond what the learner can currently do independently. The Difficulty Agent and Tutor Agent exist to maintain each child in this zone.

3. **Spaced Repetition** — skills that are practised repeatedly across sessions with increasing interval consolidate into long-term memory. The mission system and session structure are designed to enforce natural spaced repetition without making it explicit.

## Learning Domains

### Domain 1: Numeracy

**Sub-skills covered:**
- Counting (Tier 1–2)
- Addition with carrying (Tier 2–3)
- Subtraction with borrowing — change-giving (Tier 2–4)
- Multiplication — quantity × unit price (Tier 3–5)
- Division — splitting bulk orders, calculating per-unit cost (Tier 5–6)
- Fractions — half price, quarter discount (Tier 5–6)
- Percentages — 10% off, VAT concepts (Tier 6–7)
- Mental arithmetic — under time pressure from impatient customers (Tier 4–7)
- Large number fluency — working with KES 1,000, 5,000, 10,000 notes (Tier 5–7)

**How it is embedded:**
Every transaction requires calculation. The calculation is never presented as an exercise — it is the price of completing the transaction. The customer is waiting. The natural social pressure of keeping a customer waiting motivates the child without external reward structures.

### Domain 2: Literacy

**Sub-skills covered:**
- Word recognition in Swahili (Tier 1–2)
- Word recognition in English (Tier 2–3, toggle)
- Reading simple lists with quantities (Tier 2–3)
- Reading full written sentences — customer dialogue (Tier 3–5)
- Reading multi-item orders with modifiers ("2 packets of unga, the large size") (Tier 4–6)
- Reading supplier invoices — longer structured text (Tier 5–7)
- Reading the daily ledger summary — paragraph comprehension (Tier 6–7)

**How it is embedded:**
Shopping lists are the primary literacy vehicle. At the lowest tiers, they are icon-based. At middle tiers, they are handwritten-style cards with item names. At higher tiers, they are typed invoices with quantities, modifiers, and special instructions. Reading is never a standalone exercise — it is always in service of completing a transaction.

### Domain 3: Financial Literacy

**Sub-skills covered:**
- Understanding that goods have prices (Tier 1)
- Understanding that money is exchanged for goods (Tier 1–2)
- Change-giving — the buyer gives more than the price, receives the difference (Tier 2–3)
- Budgeting — managing a float to buy stock (Tier 3–5)
- Profit and loss — revenue minus costs (Tier 4–6)
- Supplier comparison — choosing between price and quantity (Tier 5–7)
- Credit — customers who owe money, tracking debt (Tier 6–7)
- Concept of margins — why a duka owner charges above cost price (Tier 6–7)
- Saving — accumulating coins toward a shop upgrade goal (all tiers)

**How it is embedded:**
The duka mechanic makes financial literacy unavoidable. A child cannot serve a customer without understanding that the item has a price. They cannot give change without understanding that the customer paid more than the price. They cannot restock without understanding that buying stock costs money. The financial concepts emerge from the game mechanic rather than being taught.

### Domain 4: Critical Thinking & Decision-Making

**Sub-skills covered:**
- Prioritisation — which item to restock first when budget is limited (Tier 4–6)
- Trade-off analysis — Supplier A vs Supplier B (Tier 5–7)
- Problem-solving — customer asks for an item that is out of stock (all tiers)
- Forecasting — estimating how much stock is needed based on mission requirements (Tier 5–7)
- Error recognition — noticing that the change total does not add up before submitting (Tier 3–6)

## Age-Band Curriculum Map

### Band 1: Sprout (Ages 4–6)

**Learning objectives:**
- Recognise that items have names and prices.
- Understand that coins have value.
- Match items on a list to items on the shelf.
- Count a small number of items (1–5).

**Game mechanics:**
- Shopping lists are icon-only — no reading required.
- Prices shown as coin piles (3 coins = KES 3).
- Change-giving replaced by coin matching — drag the right coin.
- Customer always gives exact money — no change calculation yet.
- End-of-day ledger not shown — session ends with coin celebration.

**Assessment signals for Difficulty Agent:**
- Item matching accuracy.
- Coin identification accuracy.
- Time taken per transaction.

### Band 2: Grower (Ages 7–9)

**Learning objectives:**
- Read item names in Swahili.
- Perform addition up to KES 100.
- Give change for purchases up to KES 50.
- Understand that the duka makes money from selling.

**Game mechanics:**
- Shopping lists are written Swahili words with illustrations.
- Prices up to KES 100.
- Change-giving with drag-and-drop coins (exact denominations shown).
- Simple ledger: "You made KES X today."
- First missions: "Serve 3 customers without mistakes."

**Assessment signals:**
- Reading accuracy (item selected matches list item).
- Change calculation accuracy by amount range.
- Error frequency by item type.

### Band 3: Trader (Ages 9–11)

**Learning objectives:**
- Read multi-item shopping lists with quantities.
- Multiply quantity by unit price.
- Give change using notes and coins up to KES 500.
- Manage stock — recognise when to reorder and calculate reorder cost.
- Understand profit as revenue minus cost.

**Game mechanics:**
- Shopping lists have 2–4 items with quantities ("3 packets of unga").
- Prices up to KES 500.
- Change-giving with number pad (no coin drag).
- Stock room accessible with reorder mechanic.
- Full profit/loss ledger with revenue and expenses shown separately.
- Weekly missions unlocked.

**Assessment signals:**
- Multiplication accuracy (quantity × price).
- Change accuracy by note denomination.
- Stock decision accuracy (did child reorder before running out?).

### Band 4: Entrepreneur (Ages 11–13)

**Learning objectives:**
- Perform percentage calculations (discounts, VAT).
- Compare supplier options using cost-per-unit analysis.
- Manage credit customers (track outstanding payments).
- Understand margins — why setting prices above cost matters.
- Read and interpret a full financial ledger.

**Game mechanics:**
- Shopping lists with bulk discounts ("buy 5, get 10% off").
- Two suppliers per product with different price/minimum-order trade-offs.
- Credit customer mechanic — some customers pay next time.
- Price-setting mechanic — child can adjust selling prices within a range.
- Full ledger with margin percentage shown.
- Challenge missions requiring sustained performance.

**Assessment signals:**
- Percentage calculation accuracy.
- Supplier selection optimality (did child choose the better deal?).
- Credit tracking accuracy.
- Margin decision quality.

## Skill Mastery Model

A skill is considered **in progress** when a child attempts it. It is considered **practised** when accuracy exceeds 70% over 10 attempts. It is considered **mastered** when accuracy exceeds 85% over 20 consecutive attempts with no significant regression.

The Difficulty Agent tracks skill status across three buckets per child:

| Bucket | Status | Action |
|---|---|---|
| Weak | Below 70% accuracy | Tutor Agent drills this skill via customer scenario selection |
| Practising | 70–85% accuracy | Normal distribution — appears regularly |
| Mastered | 85%+ over 20 | Appears less frequently, at higher complexity levels |

Skills never disappear from the curriculum — mastered skills are tested at higher difficulty tiers.

## Curriculum Alignment

Smart Duka aligns with the **Kenyan Competency-Based Curriculum (CBC)** for primary education:

| CBC Strand | Smart Duka Coverage |
|---|---|
| Numbers — counting and number recognition | Tier 1–2 gameplay |
| Numbers — operations (addition, subtraction) | Tier 2–4 gameplay |
| Numbers — operations (multiplication, division) | Tier 3–6 gameplay |
| Money — identifying currency | Tier 1–2 gameplay |
| Money — transactions and change | Tier 2–5 gameplay |
| Financial literacy — saving and budgeting | Tier 3–7 gameplay |
| Literacy — reading (Swahili) | All tiers (Swahili default) |
| Literacy — reading (English) | Tier 2+ (English toggle) |

This alignment enables school adoption justification and teacher confidence in recommending the product.

## What We Do Not Teach

Being explicit about scope prevents feature creep and keeps the product focused.

- We do not cover geometry, algebra, or statistics — the duka mechanic does not naturally accommodate these.
- We do not cover reading comprehension beyond functional literacy for transactions.
- We do not cover writing or composition.
- We do not cover science, history, or social studies.

These are out of scope for Version 1. Any pressure to expand scope before the core mechanic is excellent should be resisted.
